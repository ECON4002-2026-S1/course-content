1. Introduction: Why Uncertainty Matters

1.1 The Nature of Economic Decisions

Most decisions faced by economic agents—especially in agriculture and natural resources—are made under uncertainty:

DomainSources of Uncertainty
AgricultureWeather, pest outbreaks, commodity prices, input costs
FisheriesStock abundance, recruitment, catch rates, market prices
MiningOre grades, extraction costs, commodity prices, regulations
WaterRainfall, streamflow, allocation decisions, demand
EnvironmentClimate change impacts, ecosystem responses, policy shifts

1.2 Risk vs. Uncertainty: The Knight Distinction

Frank Knight (1921) distinguished between:

  • Risk: Outcomes are uncertain but probabilities are known (or can be estimated) — e.g., probability of a dry season based on historical climate data
  • Uncertainty: Outcomes are uncertain and probabilities are unknown or cannot be meaningfully assigned — e.g., probability of a completely novel regulatory change
In this course we focus primarily on risk — situations where we can assign probabilities to outcomes.

1.3 Framing Risky Decisions as Lotteries

A lottery is a formal representation of a risky prospect:

$$L = \{(x_1, p_1),\, (x_2, p_2),\, \ldots,\, (x_n, p_n)\}$$

where $x_i$ = outcome $i$, $p_i$ = probability of outcome $i$, and $\sum_{i=1}^{n} p_i = 1$. Outcomes are mutually exclusive.

2. Expected Value: A First Approach

2.1 Definition

The expected value (EV) of a lottery is the probability-weighted average of outcomes:

$$EV = E[X] = \sum_{i=1}^{n} p_i \cdot x_i$$

2.2 Farmer Fertiliser Example

A wheat farmer considers whether to apply nitrogen fertiliser. The decision depends on rainfall:

SeasonProbabilityProfit with FertiliserProfit without Fertiliser
Wet0.5$450$300
Dry0.5$50$200

Expected profit WITH fertiliser: $E[\pi_{fert}] = 0.5 \times 450 + 0.5 \times 50 = \$250$

Expected profit WITHOUT fertiliser: $E[\pi_{no}] = 0.5 \times 300 + 0.5 \times 200 = \$250$

Both strategies have the same expected profit — should the farmer be indifferent?

2.3 The Problem with Expected Value

Fertilising involves far greater variance: the outcome range is $400 (with fertiliser) vs. $100 (without). Most people prefer the less risky option when expected values are equal. This is the concept of a fair gamble: a gamble with zero expected gain. Most people are unwilling to accept fair gambles.

2.4 The St. Petersburg Paradox

The inadequacy of expected value was demonstrated by the St. Petersburg Paradox (Bernoulli, 1738):

  • A fair coin is tossed until heads appears
  • If heads appears on toss $t$, you receive $2^t$ dollars
$$EV = \frac{1}{2}(2) + \frac{1}{4}(4) + \frac{1}{8}(8) + \ldots = 1 + 1 + 1 + \ldots = \infty$$
The Paradox: Despite infinite expected value, most people would pay only $10–20 to play. People do not maximise expected value — they must be maximising something else.

3. Expected Utility Theory

3.1 The von Neumann–Morgenstern Framework

Expected Utility (EU) Theory proposes that rational decision makers maximise the expected value of a utility function over outcomes:

$$EU = E[u(X)] = \sum_{i=1}^{n} p_i \cdot u(x_i)$$
Key insight: Transform outcomes through a utility function $u(\cdot)$ before taking expectations.

3.2 Properties of Utility Functions

  1. Monotonicity: More is better → $u'(x) > 0$
  2. Risk attitude: Curvature determines attitude toward risk
  3. Cardinality: Defined only up to positive affine transformations

3.3 Solving the St. Petersburg Paradox

Using $u(x) = \ln(x)$:

$$EU = \sum_{t=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{2^t} \ln(2^t) = \sum_{t=1}^{\infty} \frac{t \ln 2}{2^t} = 2\ln 2 \approx 1.39$$

A finite expected utility! The certainty equivalent is $e^{1.39} \approx \$4$.

3.4 Farmer Example with Expected Utility

Suppose the farmer has utility function $u(x) = 10\sqrt{x}$:

With fertiliser:

$$EU_{fert} = 0.5 \times 10\sqrt{450} + 0.5 \times 10\sqrt{50} = 106.07 + 35.36 = 141.42$$

Without fertiliser:

$$EU_{no} = 0.5 \times 10\sqrt{300} + 0.5 \times 10\sqrt{200} = 86.60 + 70.71 = 157.31$$

Decision: $EU_{no} > EU_{fert}$ → the farmer should NOT fertilise.

Note: $u(250) = 10\sqrt{250} = 158.11 > EU_{fert} = 141.42$, confirming the farmer prefers certainty over the fertiliser gamble.

4. Certainty Equivalent and Risk Premium

4.1 Certainty Equivalent (CE)

The certainty equivalent is the guaranteed amount that yields the same utility as a risky lottery:

$$u(CE) = EU \implies CE = u^{-1}(EU)$$

For the fertiliser lottery:

$$10\sqrt{CE} = 141.42 \implies CE = 200$$

The farmer is indifferent between the risky fertiliser lottery and a guaranteed $200.

4.2 Risk Premium (RP)

The risk premium is the amount the decision maker would pay to eliminate risk:

$$RP = E[X] - CE$$

For the fertiliser lottery: $RP = 250 - 200 = \$50$

The farmer would pay up to $50 to convert the risky prospect into a certain $200.

5. Risk Attitudes

5.1 Three Types of Decision Makers

Risk AttitudeUtility Shape$u''(x)$Behaviour
Risk AverseConcave$u'' < 0$Rejects fair gambles; $CE < EV$
Risk NeutralLinear$u'' = 0$Indifferent to fair gambles; $CE = EV$
Risk LovingConvex$u'' > 0$Accepts fair gambles; $CE > EV$

5.2 Mathematical Characterisation

Risk aversion is equivalent to:

  1. Concave utility: $u''(x) < 0$ (diminishing marginal utility)
  2. Jensen's Inequality: $E[u(X)] < u(E[X])$ for non-degenerate $X$
  3. Positive risk premium: $RP > 0$
  4. Fair gamble rejection: Declines any gamble with $E[\Delta W] = 0$

5.4 Arrow–Pratt Measures of Risk Aversion

Absolute Risk Aversion (ARA):

$$A(x) = -\frac{u''(x)}{u'(x)}$$

Relative Risk Aversion (RRA):

$$R(x) = -\frac{x \cdot u''(x)}{u'(x)} = x \cdot A(x)$$

Example for $u(x) = \ln(x)$: $u'(x) = 1/x,\ u''(x) = -1/x^2$ → $A(x) = 1/x$ (decreasing), $R(x) = 1$ (constant RRA).

With CRRA utility, the percentage of wealth allocated to risky assets is constant regardless of wealth level.

6. The Value of Information

6.1 Value of Perfect Information (VPI)

The VPI is the maximum amount a decision maker would pay to know the true state before making a decision.

  • Without information: choose the action that maximises EU given prior probabilities
  • With information: choose the optimal action conditional on each state
$$VPI = CE_{PI} - CE_{\text{no info}}$$

6.2 Farmer Example: Value of Weather Forecast

SeasonProbProfit (Fert)Profit (No Fert)Optimal
Wet0.5$450$300Fertilise
Dry0.5$50$200Don't fertilise

Without information: Optimal is not to fertilise → $EU = 157.31$, $CE = \$247.50$

With perfect information:

  • If Wet → Fertilise → $u(450) = 212.13$
  • If Dry → Don't fertilise → $u(200) = 141.42$
  • $EU_{PI} = 0.5 \times 212.13 + 0.5 \times 141.42 = 176.78$, $CE_{PI} = \$312.50$
$$VPI = 312.50 - 247.50 = \$65$$

The farmer would pay up to $65 for perfect weather forecasting.

6.3 Imperfect Information

In practice, information is imperfect. The value of imperfect information lies between zero and VPI, and is calculated using Bayesian updating of probabilities — see Practice Problem 11 for a worked example.

7. Applications in Agricultural and Resource Economics

7.1 Crop Insurance

Insurance contract: premium $P$ (paid upfront); indemnity pays $(Y^* - Y)$ if yield $Y < Y^*$.

Fair premium: $P_{\text{fair}} = E[\text{Indemnity}]$

Key result: Risk-averse farmers are willing to pay more than the fair premium. The excess represents the risk premium they pay for certainty.

7.2 Wheat Crop Insurance Example

WeatherProbYield (t/ha)Revenue (@$300/t)
Good0.43.0$900
Average0.42.0$600
Drought0.20.5$150

Without insurance ($u(x) = \sqrt{x}$): $EU = 24.25$, $CE = \$588$

Insurance contract guaranteeing 2 t/ha: fair premium $P_{\text{fair}} = 0.2 \times 450 = \$90$

With fair insurance ($P = 90$): $EU = 24.93$, $CE = \$622$

Welfare gain: $622 - 588 = \$34$. Maximum premium: $\approx \$124$ (the farmer pays up to $34 above fair premium).

7.3 Crop Portfolio Diversification

A farmer allocates 100 ha between two crops (wheat: $E = \$400$/ha, $\sigma = \$200$; canola: $E = \$500$/ha, $\sigma = \$300$; $\rho = 0.3$).

Portfolio expected return: $E[R_p] = 400w + 500(100-w)$

Portfolio variance:

$$\sigma_p^2 = w^2 \sigma_W^2 + (100-w)^2 \sigma_C^2 + 2w(100-w)\rho\sigma_W\sigma_C$$

A risk-averse farmer may allocate some area to wheat (lower return, lower variance) even though canola has higher expected return, to reduce overall portfolio risk.

7.4 Water Allocation Under Uncertainty

Water managers face uncertain inflows. The state-contingent framework maximises expected welfare across inflow states subject to water availability in each state.

7.5 Fisheries and the Precautionary Approach

Under stock uncertainty, a risk-averse regulator applies:

$$H^* = E[MSY] - k \cdot \sigma_{MSY}$$

where $k > 0$ reflects risk aversion — reducing expected catch to lower the probability of stock collapse.

8. Advanced Topics

8.1 Mean–Variance Analysis

When returns are normally distributed (or utility is quadratic):

$$EU \approx E[W] - \frac{A}{2}\text{Var}(W)$$

Decision makers trade off expected return against variance, with the trade-off governed by absolute risk aversion $A$.

8.2 Stochastic Dominance

First-Order Stochastic Dominance (FOSD): Lottery $A$ FOSD-dominates $B$ if $F_A(x) \leq F_B(x)$ for all $x$ — all agents who prefer more prefer $A$.

Second-Order Stochastic Dominance (SOSD): $A$ SOSD-dominates $B$ if $\int_{-\infty}^{x} F_A(t)\,dt \leq \int_{-\infty}^{x} F_B(t)\,dt$ for all $x$ — all risk-averse agents prefer $A$.

8.3 Behavioural Departures from EU Theory

PhenomenonDescriptionExample
Certainty effectOverweight certain outcomesPrefer sure $3,000 over 80% chance of $4,000
Loss aversionLosses loom larger than gainsRefuse a fair 50/50 gamble of ±$100
Probability weightingOverweight small probabilitiesBuy lottery tickets; over-insure rare events
Framing effectsChoice depends on presentation"90% survival" vs. "10% mortality"
Prospect Theory (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979) addresses these violations by evaluating outcomes relative to a reference point, weighting probabilities non-linearly, and using a kinked value function (steeper for losses).

9.1 Core Formulas

ConceptFormula
Expected Value$EV = \sum_i p_i x_i$
Expected Utility$EU = \sum_i p_i u(x_i)$
Certainty Equivalent$u(CE) = EU \implies CE = u^{-1}(EU)$

9.2 Risk Aversion Conditions (All Equivalent)

  1. Concave utility: $u''(x) < 0$
  2. Positive risk premium: $RP > 0$
  3. Certainty equivalent below expected value: $CE < EV$
  4. Rejects fair gambles
  5. $E[u(X)] < u(E[X])$ (Jensen's Inequality)
