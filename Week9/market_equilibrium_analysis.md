
# Market Equilibrium Analysis

## Part I

### Market Types
- Perfectly competitive
- Monopoly/Monopsony
- Duopoly, Oligopoly

### Competitive Markets
- Consumers and producers take the prices of goods and services as given and outside their control.
- An individual economic agent's actions have no effect on market prices.
- Individual agent optimizing behavior takes price as exogenous, although the agents collectively determine the market price.

### Competitive Firm
- Maximizes profits:
  $$\max_{y} \pi = p y - c(y)$$
- First Order Condition (F.O.C) and Second Order Condition (S.O.C):
  $$p = c'(y*)$$
  $$c''(y) \geq 0$$
- Marginal cost pricing is optimal: $p = MC$ and MC is increasing.

### Supply
- For a competitive firm $i$, the marginal cost curve above the average cost curve is the supply curve: $y_i(p)$
- Industry supply curve:
  $$S(p) = \sum_{i} y_i(p)$$
- Horizontal aggregation of supply.

### Equilibrium
- Individual consumers are usually or almost always price takers.
- Market equilibrium: supply equals demand:
  $$S(p) = \sum_{i} y_i(p) = \sum_{i} x_i(p) = D(p)$$

### Equilibrium and Welfare
- Market outcome is Pareto optimal.
- Social welfare is maximized: Consumer Surplus (CS) + Producer Surplus (PS).
- Intervention in the market reduces efficiency.

### Limitations of the Model
- Market equilibrium adjustment is not instantaneous.
- Information and transaction costs can prevent the market from operating optimally.
- Equilibrium outcomes can be stable or unstable.
- Market failure is assumed away.
- Markets are not relevant for the allocation of goods and services that do not go through the market.
- Efficiency does not mean equity or distributional justice.

## Part II

### Market: Entry and Exit
- Firms will enter when profits are positive and exit when they are negative.
- Equilibrium when profits are zero.
- Enough firms in the industry so that prices are equal to the minimum average cost.

#### Policy: Subsidy Analysis
- Government subsidizes at the rate of $1 per unit produced and sold.
- Subsidy raises producer price while lowering consumer price.
- Welfare effects:
  - Change in Consumer Surplus: $+a$
  - Change in Producer Surplus: $+b$
  - Subsidy paid: $a + b + c$
  - Deadweight loss: $c$
